# Analysis
 
Business insights from the dashboard, organized into its four sections:
Key Metrics, Segment Performance, Monthly Metrics, and 4Ps.
 
## 1. Key Metrics
 
| Metric | YoY Change (Jan–Jul 2025 vs. 2024) |
|---|---|
| Orders | ▼ 33% |
| Sales | ▼ 29% |
| Profit | ▼ 27% |
 
**Insight:** All three metrics declined together — this is a broad downturn
across the business, not an issue isolated to pricing, cost, or a single
product line.
 
## 2. Segment Performance
 
| Segment | Orders ('24→'25) | Revenue ('24→'25) | Profit ('24→'25) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Professional | 38 → 20 (▼47%) | $6,267 → $2,603 (▼58%) | $2,859 → $1,115 (▼61%) |
| Student | 55 → 24 (▼56%) | $6,612 → $2,837 (▼57%) | $2,834 → $1,349 (▼52%) |
| Parent | 33 → 19 (▼42%) | $3,669 → $2,981 (▼19%) | $1,601 → $1,354 (▼15%) |
| Teacher | 13 → 4 (▼69%) | $1,591 → $302 (▼81%) | $736 → $153 (▼79%) |
| Retiree | 8 → 6 (▼25%) | $601 → $1,133 (▲89%) | $313 → $522 (▲67%) |
 
**Insight:** Professional and Student were the two highest-revenue segments
in 2024 and now account for the largest absolute revenue lost — this is
where a recovery campaign has the biggest upside. Parent is comparatively
resilient (small revenue/profit drop despite fewer orders). Teacher is the
weakest performer on every metric. Retiree is the outlier: order volume
fell but revenue and profit both grew, pointing to higher spend per
purchase.
 
## 3. Monthly Metrics
 
| Segment | Peak | Low | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Professional | Jul, Oct 2024 | Jan–Feb 2025 | Quarterly / pay-cycle rhythm |
| Student | Jan, Sep 2024 | Mar–Apr 2025 | Academic-term cycle |
| Parent | Sep–Oct 2024 | Jan–Feb 2025 | Tied to school/festive periods |
| Teacher | Jan, Jul 2024 | Jan–Apr 2025 | Flat, low responsiveness |
| Retiree | May 2024 | Jan–Feb 2025 | Declining volume, but larger orders |
 
**Insight:** Each segment buys on a different calendar — Students around
semester starts, Professionals around quarterly pay cycles, Parents around
school terms. A single always-on campaign would miss most of these windows;
timing needs to be segment-specific.
 
## 4. 4Ps (Place, Product, Promotion, Price)
 
| Segment | Place | Product | Promotion | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Professional | Online (38%), QLD (20%), VIC (18%) | Fiction, Textbook, Non-Fiction | 64% no discount — low price sensitivity | 65% in $0–50, 19% in $100–150 |
| Student | NSW (37%), Online (29%) | Non-Fiction, Fiction, Textbook | Responds to Student10 / Member15 codes | 73% under $50 |
| Parent | Online (24%), VIC (24%), NSW (22%) | Textbook (30%), Non-Fiction, Fiction | 69% no discount | 60% in $0–50, 21% in $50–100 |
| Teacher | Online (53%), NSW/VIC (35%) | Children's, Fiction | 53% no discount; minor uptake of Member15/Student10 | 71% under $50 |
| Retiree | Online (38%), VIC (23%) | Fiction, Non-Fiction, Textbook | 57% no discount | 61% under $50, 19% in $100–150 |
 
**Insight:** Online is the dominant channel for every segment — any recovery
campaign should be digital-first. Most segments show low reliance on
discounts (majority of orders use none), except Students, who respond
clearly to promo codes — suggesting price-based incentives work for
Students specifically, while other segments respond better to
value/loyalty framing than to discounting.
